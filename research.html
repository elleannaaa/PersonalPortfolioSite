Throughout my time in the Honors Program, I have taken a variety of different courses that have expanded my knowledge on intersectionality, the focus of the program. After completing our desired courses, we then get to decide on a research topic of our choice. Given my passion for cats, evolutionary biology, and feminist theory, I composed my thesis abstract:

"Many ethical frameworks have been proposed throughout the course of human history, but history extends far before and likely far beyond homo sapiens. Traditionally, the realm of ethical thought and scholarship has been confined to humans, but non-human beings arguably have their own ethics that arise out of biological necessity and socialization. For example, feral and free roaming cats form complex social structures amongst each other and with humans which are governed by specific sets of social norms and routines. Through the use of animal behavioral science, evolutionary biology, feminist theory, and original observational research, this capstone seeks to define an ethic of the Feral Cat, both to highlight the agency and contributions of non-human teachers as well as to contribute to the expansion of human ethical and philosophical knowledge."
